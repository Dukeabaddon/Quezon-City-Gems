U.P. Diliman

Address: Roxas Ave, Diliman, Quezon City

History:

The University of the Philippines was established in 1908 by Act No. 1870 of the Philippine Legislature to “give advanced instruction in literature, philosophy, the sciences and arts, and to give professional and technical training to every qualified student regardless of age, sex, nationality, religious belief and political affiliation.” Initially, UP had three colleges: the College of Fine Arts, the College of Liberal Arts, and the College of Medicine and Surgery in Manila. As the student population continued to increase, the need for more buildings and academic units became apparent. In 1939, the Board of Regents acquired 493-hectares of land in Diliman, Quezon City. Construction began in the same year but the development of the area was stalled by World War II. The university administration led by UP President Bienvenido Gonzales sought a P13 million-grant from the US-Philippines War Damage Commission to restore the damaged facilities.


Facts and Trivia:

The University of the Philippines Diliman (UPD) is the main campus of the University of the Philippines System (UP System), the national university by virtue of Republic Act 9500. It is renowned for its academic excellence and provides a wide range of undergraduate, graduate, and professional programs in a variety of fields. It has also played a significant role in the history of the Philippines, often serving as a center for intellectual and political activity as well as a site for various historical events and protests. The university is home to numerous research centers, institutes, and libraries that contribute to the country's academic and scientific advancements. Plus, it has produced number of notable alumni, including leaders, scholars, artists, and public figures who have made significant contributions to society.


Additional Information:

UP Diliman is home to the "UP Oblation," a famous and iconic sculpture located in the heart of the campus. The Oblation is a symbol of selfless service and sacrifice for the sake of the greater good. This iconic statue has become a symbol of the University of the Philippines and is often associated with the spirit of academic excellence, nationalism, and social responsibility that the university upholds.
